On Methodology

3 December, 2024

With a subject as complex and consequential as life guidance, it is no surprise that there are almost as many views on what the correct approach is as there are those offering to provide them—and that those viewpoints are debated furiously. My work is very different from conventional coaching, psychotherapy, or counselling, and is characterized by three pillars:

  • (i) Scientific, peer-reviewed findings about psychosocial root cause,
  • (ii) An emphasis on the importance of potentially uncomfortable self-exploration, and
  • (iii) Actionable steps, designed to be taken outside of sessions, aimed at subconscious growth and transformation.

All three pillars exist in the context of the emotional openness, care, humor, and responsibility I hope to bring to my client relationships as a counsellor.

Specifically, I practice a model of coaching and counselling, that work in concert. Borrowing from the popular adage, "give a person a fish, and you feed them for a day; but teach a person to fish, and you feed them for a lifetime," I view 'counselling' as the process of giving the client fish, and 'coaching' as teaching them to fish for themselves.

I am a counsellor inasmuch as I invite you to come to sessions as you are, and to view our sessions as compassionate, non-judgmental spaces where we work together through anything and everything that you choose to bring. I am a coach, in that my primary goal is to design a program that helps you move towards your goals, and feel fulfilled on a fundamental, emotional level. At the point where you are satisfied of your ability to be an efficacious coach to yourself, I will encourage you to terminate our sessions together.

A key concept here is 're-parenting'; i.e., learning to use one's rational, conscious faculties—which are within direct control—to re-wire the subconscious (pre-rational) minds towards capacity and health. As the conscious mind can only out-will or overpower the subconscious for very short bursts of time, this last step—alongside the rigorous empirical grounding of all that I teach, and my emphasis on the importance of conscious self-knowledge—is perhaps most defining of the work I do, vis-à-vis conventional psychotherapy or life coaching.

In order to do the work of self-understanding and re-parenting, I employ frameworks drawn from empirical study (such as Integrated Attachment Theory, CBT, DBT, IFS, and the work of the Gottman Institute), and insights on how our minds operate, taken from the latest research in cognitive neuroscience and trauma-informed psychotherapeutic methods. I am agnostic as to the ideological or cultural source of the teaching and wisdom I espouse, so long as the concept can be demonstrated empirically as statistically significant, or by a process of logical proof. In this regard—and without meaning to demean or detract from the potential insights enabled by other methods—my approach differs most obviously from the guidance one might receive from theological sources, executive coaches, life coaches, 'new age' alternatives, or, indeed, from conventional Western talk therapists.

An analogy that I use to describe why I believe a critical approach to truth-claims is important to helping clients meet their goals is that of auto mechanics. Imagine a car rolls into a mechanic's shop, and the driver reports that something is wrong with it. A passable mechanic might conduct a limited diagnostic, and draw a conclusion that points to a superficial fix to a superficial problem. This might work temporarily, but unless the problem at the very lowest level of causality was addressed, the owner might be able to drive the car away with the issue ameliorated, but they would need to keep bringing it back into the shop eventually. This dynamic is how I would describe my experience with conventional Western psychotherapy.

A truly skilled mechanic, however, who has extensive training and knowledge about how vehicles work, would be able to isolate with as high a degree of accuracy as possible what the root cause of the problem is. Knowing the nature of the problem that exists at the lowest possible level inside-and-out, they'd be able to conduct whatever fixes were necessary to get the car running again. And, because they'd fixed this problem many times before, they'd be able to use their experience with this type of issue to do the job efficiently, without damaging other sensitive components as they complete it.

I entered this field because I discovered that—unlike good auto mechanics—most psychotherapists, coaches, or counsellors do not hold themselves to the standard of understanding root cause at its lowest possible level. Psychodynamics, psychiatry, and psychoanalysis—not to mention various forms of new-age healing, or life coaching—rely on tools that might do more good than harm, but that do not get to the heart of the matter. An example of this might be: How do we know that talking about a traumatic event is palliative? Because Freud said so (appeal to authority). Or, because people seem to feel better afterwards (false cause). I insist on an academic's approach to the acceptance or rejection of a theory: Is it explanatory? Does it have robust predictive power? Is it parsimonious? And, is it falsifiable? Clients that seek out my services tend to be looking not only for a caring and emotionally open counsellor, but also a skilled practitioner, and an exacting thinker, who takes justification of truth-claims seriously.

An exhaustive attempt to describe and compare the major therapies readily available in countries such as the United States and Germany would be the subject of a doctoral dissertation, so I limit myself here to an overview and comparison of the Integrated Attachment approach that I practice, against two major approaches in the Western tradition: Psychotherapy, and Psychiatry.

Emerging in Europe in the 19th Century and refined among the European diaspora in North America in the 20th, both Psychotherapy and Psychiatry are inextricably tied to European and Judeo-Christian cultural approaches to psychosocial health. Put very generally, Europeans would historically deal with their emotional pain by talking about it with their priest or rabbi, and/or drinking their sorrows away at the local tavern. Religious confession and attenant pastoral care—or traditions surrounding drinking as social lubricant, pasttime, and spiritual aid—served as the dual-pronged mechanism by which Western Europeans would deal with their suffering. Contemporary Psychotherapy been tempered by Psychoanalysis, and in its time, Psychodynamics, however it is still fundamentally of the same form as a Christian confession: It is discursive, and employs narrative ways of understanding its subject matter that rest, at their core, on faith (even if, now, the Godhead is no longer the Trinity or Scripture, but is Freud, Jung, or the inventor of a particular psychodynamic technique).

Contemporary Psychiatry, on the other hand, emerged alongside late 20th Century advances in encephalography, neurotransmitter research, and genomics, that allowed scientists to measure electrical and chemical activity in the brain. As what gets measured gets managed, neuroscientists latched on to what data they had about how the mind works to create the advanced antipsychotic and antidepressant drugs we see marketed today. However, Psychiatry's approach to dealing with emotional and spiritual problems is unchanged from its pre-modern roots: Both alcohol and Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors ('SSRIs') such as Prozac, for example, are fundamentally psychotropic drugs that temporarily manipulate brain chemistry to create symptomatic relief.

Although the subject of the now secularized pastoral discussions and the composition of the drugs that are prescribed to alleviate 'diseases' of mental health have become more advanced, as potential palliatives to psychosocial trauma and aids to emotional education, Psychotherapy and Psychiatry retain the same fundamental shortcomings as their precursors. They fail to accurately address the root causes of emotional distress and dysfunction, and to offer lasting solutions that target these root issues.

Western psychodynamic psychotherapy traces its lineage to the work of Austrian philosopher and neurologist Sigmund Freud, who believed that much of human thought, emotion, and behavior is influenced by unconscious processes. His research and writing fundamentally challenged the European Enlightenment-era (Cartesian, Kantian) view of humans as fully rational beings. A clinician as well as a philosopher, Freud enumerated an elaborate theory of the psyche (introducing such terms as Id, Ego, and Superego)

Currently, Psychodynamics has moved well beyond Freud, who died in Britain in 1939. However, the practice of Psychodynamic Psychotherapy continues to take as the answer to the questions, 'well, how do we know that this principle holds?', or, 'how do we know that this process works?', as fundamentally uninteresting: the appeal is to the authority of the thinker, such as Freud, or Swiss and French psychiatrists respectively, Carl Jung, or Jacques Lacan—or to the government, i.e., the university that taught the course, or to the institutional body that granted the license to practice.

To be clear, I am not making the argument that Psychodynamic Psychotherapy is wrong. It is entirely possible that a practice without a rigorous relationship to truth-claims can still have something useful to offer. It is abundantly clear to me, for instance, that adherents to religious programs, or to 'alternative' methods gain some value from them. I am, however, suggesting that my coaching and counselling practice is different from all of the above in that it insists on a critical relationship to etiology and foundational truth. This makes my science-based coaching and counselling approach more suitable to those who, perhaps, are not as good a fit for traditional religions, or who have tried conventional psychotherapy but find it's not as results-oriented as they would like—and, ultimately, who may care more about knowing that what they're being taught in session is the product of peer-review in an academic journal.
