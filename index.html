Hi! I'm Aadit. I help people find fulfilment in work, love, and life.

My coaching and counselling practice is informed by findings of modern neuroscientific research and developmental psychology; a focus on root cause rather than symptom or sign; and the joy I find supporting my clients as they ask and answer difficult questions about life. Focus Areas:

Self

Creating a healthy, self-understanding, and capable relationship to oneself and one's own emotions is critical to a life well-lived.

Foci include: Transforming depression, intrusive thinking, and sadness; eliminating inner guilt and shame; discovering and embracing one’s unique needs and values; breaking through self-sabotage and procrastination; and overcoming anxiety, for presence and peace-of-mind. Read more →

Others

The European Psychoanalyst Sigmund Freud believed that of all forms of suffering, that which we feel in our relationships with others is the most devastating.

I take a results-based approach to helping clients build thriving bonds with others. Together, we explore topics such as self-esteem, healthy and skilled conflict resolution, ending codependency and enmeshment, how to heal from breakups, and how to transform grief and loss. Some clients want to dive deeply into how to release resentment from relationships, and truly forgive; overcome loneliness or social anxiety; or master the fear of rejection. Read more →

Career

In the 25 or so wealthiest countries in the world, and amongst the quickly growing middle- and upper classes across much of the rest of the planet, the 'problem of plenty' that has plagued humanity for the vast majority of our evolutionary history has largely been solved.

However, though we have enough food, water, and shelter to go around—for the most part and for most people—true fulfillment in a job and career seems no closer at hand. But this needn't be the case, and finding it is an eminently solvable problem—if only we are given the tools, often not taught in school or by our childhood caregivers, to systematically work through the internal and external barriers that stand in the way of the careers of our dreams. About Me:

I began my training as a psychologist in Integrated Attachment Theory (IAT) with Dr. Thais Gibson in 2020, and completed my certification as an IAT coach in early 2024. Prior to opening my counseling and coaching private practice, I worked as a corporate strategy consultant in New York City, and started a concurrent research appointment with behavioral economists Dr. Karla Hoff and Nobel-prizewinner Dr. Joseph Stiglitz at Columbia University.

Although I have an extensive background in academia and in industry, I find the most joy and purpose working one-on-one with people seeking support in a coaching and counselling context. My prior training and continued intellectual interests in psychology, psychotherapy, trauma studies, philosophy, and social thought undergird the work that I do with my clients.

❊

The journey that brought me to this work began when I was a patient in conventional psychotherapy—posessing the outward trappings of a high paying job and prestigious education, but struggling with subterranean aimlessness, depression, anxiety, and painful patterns in relationships.

Though therapy was marginally helpful, I was frustrated that I was spending tens of thousands of dollars and many hundreds of hours going around in circles, not getting the depth of support I felt I needed, and failing to make tangible progress towards my goals in work and love. Discovering the community of developmental psychopathologists that included Dr. Bessel van der Kolk of Boston University's School of Medicine, Dr. Gabor Maté, and Dr. Dante Cicchetti of Harvard and the University of Rochester—and Dr. Gibson's approach to targeted interventions at the subconscious level of mind—proved to be an inflection point, and set me on the course to the work I do today.

❊

I spend most of my time between New York City, Berlin, and Bangalore; though my heart lies in the wildernesses of Vermont and the California Eastern Sierra. I grew up in Indonesia, the Philippines, Vietnam, and Myanmar—as a child of expat parents from India—and attended international schools with kids from all over the world.

I was educated at the United World College of South-East Asia, Yale-NUS College, Deep Springs College, and Columbia University.

Frequently Asked Questions:

Are you a 'therapist'? Do you offer psychotherapy?

In the United States, a 'psychotherapist' is an individual who has been licensed by their State to practice mental health counseling. I am not a psychotherapist. However, there are broad overlaps in the goals I and a psychotherapist might have in our work with you. Please see On Methodology → to read more about the similarities and differences between conventional psychotherapy and what I offer.

Why did you not choose not to pursue psychotherapeutic licensure in New York State?

In short, because degree programs that terminate in licensure do not necessarily equip putative therapists with the skills they need to succeed. Currently, vocational degree programs in mental health counseling in the United States do not necessarily teach licensees how to lead healthier lives, build deep and trusting relationships, process their emotions skilfully, navigate grief or death; nor do they give licensees the pedagogical tools to teach their clients and patients how to do so. The degree-granting programs that exist in Western countries are a product of their zeitgeist: A medicalized culture that focusses on pharmaceuticals to deal with outward symptoms of mental ill-health, and that treats the emotional and spiritual substance of life as beyond the pale of scientific study. Course content that can loosely be described as dealing with root cause focuses primarily on a circumscribed set of psychologic and psychotherapeutic tools (such as Cognitive Behavioral Therapy, or 20th Century Developmental Psychology); an understanding of the legal and ethical structure that regulates licensed clinical care; and the principles of social work—not on the multi-faceted and deeply introspective process necessary to sufficiently equip people with a robust emotional education, the tools to heal from traumatic pasts, or answer some of the thorniest questions in life. The expectation in the U.S. model is that clinicians, once licensed, pursue certifications and continuing education to help them do their jobs.

Beyond my education as a psychologist and philosopher of science at Columbia, I am certified in Integrated Attachment Theory coaching—and as part of the certification process, was expected to learn from many of the best models, certifications, and schools of thought that conventional therapists also draw on. These include CBT, DBT, and IFS. However, being untethered from the yoke of 20th Century psychiatrics, IAT also incorporates findings from 21st Century neuroscience relating to neuroplasticity, the collapsing of mind-body duality, research on nervous system function, science-backed Mindfulness-based modalities, and the empirical observations of researchers such as those at the Gottman Institute and the Trauma Research Foundation.

To sum up, poetically: I chose to enter this career because I was not able to find a therapist or traditional counselor who could help me, when I was at my moment of deepest need. The frustration I had with the system as a patient set me on the course to take my healing into my own hands, and to do the work I do today, with clients seeking impactful insights and real results.

Whose academic work are you particularly inspired by?

Dr. Gabor Maté, Dr. Bessel van der Kolk, Dr. Thais Gibson, Dr. Stephen Porges, Dr. Dante Cicchetti, Dr. Antonio Damasio, Alain de Botton, Dr. Richard Schwartz, and Dr. Ron Siegel, among others.

How many sessions will we need together?

The answer is highly dependent on your goals, and where you are on your journey towards the ideal outcomes in major areas of your life (Relationships, Career, Financial, Emotional/Spiritual, Mental/Intellectual and Physical Health/Environment). For a rough range, however: Some clients with specific focus areas are satisfied with eight sessions, others, who want to do deep and holistic work across all areas choose to do between 20 and 30 sessions in total, over the course of six months to a year.

Coaching and counseling is time-bound. We will design a program together in our first few sessions, which will give us a clearer idea of how long the process is estimated to take. Once you are satisfied that you are sufficiently equipped, I will encourage you to terminate our sessions together and apply your new skills and knowledge on your own terms!

Why did you decide become a counselor and coach?

I emerged into young adulthood suffering from depression, anxiety, a lack of authentic direction in my career, and painful patterns in my intimate relationships. I needed to figure out how to live a good life from scratch. I spent many years in therapy, finding out that conventional psychotherapy was not a good fit, and conventional psychopharmacology was at best a way to alleviate the symptoms of deeply-rooted yet eminently solveable emotional and life challenges. I needed something I believed was more results-oriented and effective in its treatment, and rigorous in establishing truth-claims (i.e., in answering the questions: What is at the root of the problem? And, how can we establish that our claim is true, scientifically?). I subsequently decided to apply my education in philosophy and the natural sciences to trauma studies—learning about the Psychotherapeutic Method from scratch—and to Integrated Attachment Theory.

Initially, I embarked on this journey because I needed to support myself, and transform my own life. Now, many years later, I want to put all that I've learned to use in also serving and supporting others.

In a nutshell, what is the goal of our work together?

To do the subconscious healing work and practical skills-building required to develop thriving, secure, and deep bonds with others; a healthy, compassionate, and illuminated relationship to yourself; and to equip you to deal with the inevitable difficulties of living even the best and most successful life with calm, charm, and wisdom.
